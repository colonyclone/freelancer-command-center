Why a Notion client tracker beats a spreadsheet

Spreadsheets are fine for a list of names and emails. But the moment you want to see a client's projects, invoices, and meeting notes in one place — without copy-pasting data between four sheets — they fall apart.

Notion's database system solves this with relations and rollups: you can link a client record to their projects, and then their projects to their invoices, and all of that information flows through automatically. Open a client and see everything about them without leaving the page.

The other advantage: Notion is free. A dedicated CRM like HoneyBook costs $19/month. Notion's free plan handles everything a solo freelancer needs.

Setting up views that actually help you

A Notion database is only as useful as its views. The same client data can be arranged in completely different ways — and having the right view open at the right time is what makes the difference between a system you use and one you abandon.

Here are the four views to set up in your client tracker:

1. All Clients (default table view)

Your master list. Show all properties. Sort by Status, then by Monthly Value descending. This is what you open when you need to look something up about a specific client.

2. Active Clients (filtered table)

Filter: Status = Active. This is your working view — the clients you're currently serving. Keep this one bookmarked. When a client project ends, change their status to Completed and they disappear from this view automatically.

3. By Status (grouped table)

Group by: Status. See all your clients organized into groups: Leads, Active, Paused, Completed. Useful for the big-picture review of your client base — monthly or quarterly.

4. High Value (sorted table)

Sort by: Monthly Value descending. No filter. This tells you at a glance who your most valuable clients are, which matters when you're deciding where to invest your energy and who to prioritize during busy periods.

Linking clients to projects and invoices

This is where Notion's power really shows. Once you've built a basic Projects database and an Invoices database, you can link them to your Clients database using relation properties.

Here's how it works: in your Clients database, add a property of type Relation and point it at your Projects database. Notion creates a two-way link — when you link a project to a client, the client record shows that project, and the project record shows the client.

Add a Rollup property on top of that relation to do calculations across linked records — for example, summing all invoice amounts linked to a client to show their total revenue earned.

The result: open any client record and you see:

  • Their active and past projects (with status and due dates)
  • All invoices (with paid/unpaid status)
  • Total revenue earned (automatically calculated)

You never have to switch databases to understand a client's full picture.

Step-by-step: build your client tracker in 20 minutes

1

Create a new page in Notion

Click + New page in your sidebar. Title it "Clients". Click the table icon under "Database" to create a new full-page table database. This is your client tracker.

2

Add your properties

Click the + to the right of the last column header to add each property. Add in order: Status (Select), Email (Email), Phone (Phone), Industry (Select), Monthly Value (Number — format as currency), Start Date (Date), Notes (Text). Don't add relations yet.

3

Set up your Status options

Click the Status property header → Edit property. Add these options with colors: Lead (gray), Active (green), Paused (yellow), Completed (blue), Churned (red). Color-coding makes your Kanban and grouped views instantly readable.

4

Add 3–5 real clients

Don't leave it empty — populate it with your actual current clients now. Even just name + status + email. The system only becomes useful when it has real data. Delete the sample "Untitled" row Notion creates by default.

5

Create your views

Click + Add a view at the top left. Create the four views described in the previous section: All Clients (table), Active Clients (filtered table), By Status (grouped), High Value (sorted). Name them clearly.

6

Bookmark the Active Clients view

This is your daily view. Right-click the page in your sidebar and choose Add to Favorites. Switch to your Active Clients view before bookmarking so it opens on that view by default.

7

Add relations (once you have a Projects database)

Create a Projects database in a new Notion page. Then come back to Clients, add a new property → Relation → select Projects. Do the same for an Invoices database. Now open any client row and you can link their projects directly from inside the client record.

Pro tip: Open a client's record (click on the row) and use the full-page view to write notes from calls, attach files, and see their linked projects. Notion page records are full documents — your client tracker doubles as a client note-taking system.

FAQ

Do I need a paid Notion plan?

No. Notion's free plan is sufficient for a solo freelancer's client tracker. The only limitation on the free plan is that you can't invite guests to edit (only view), and you're limited to 10 MB per file upload. Neither affects this use case.

Can I use this on mobile?

Yes. Notion has iOS and Android apps. The mobile experience is decent for viewing and quick updates. Heavy data entry (adding many new clients or properties) is easier on desktop.

How do I migrate from my current spreadsheet?

Notion supports CSV import. Export your spreadsheet as a CSV, then in Notion: New page → Import → CSV. It creates a database from your CSV columns automatically. You'll need to convert some columns to the right property types (e.g., make the Status column a Select property), but the data transfers cleanly.

Can I share my client database with a VA or team member?

Yes. On Notion's free plan you can share a page with up to 10 guests (view or edit access). Paid plans remove this limit. For most solo freelancers with a part-time VA, the free plan works fine.

What if I want clients to see their own project info?

That's what the Client Welcome Kit template is for. It's a separate shareable Notion page — a client portal — that you duplicate per client and share a link to. The client sees their project milestones, file hub, and communication guidelines without accessing your internal database.

Is this better than Dubsado or HoneyBook?

Depends on what you need. HoneyBook and Dubsado automate things Notion can't: sending contracts, collecting e-signatures, automated invoice reminders, and payment processing. If you need those automations, they're worth the $19-20/month. If you mainly need clarity and organization — and you already use separate tools for contracts and payments — Notion is more flexible and free.
